Our Analysis

In the beginning of the video, right after answering the question about whether women are happier as housewives, Andrew Tate immediately shifts the focus to a different topic: he begins talking about how “giving life is one of the most beautiful things in the world.”

Now, on the surface, this is a statement many people can agree with. Giving life—childbirth, motherhood, and the nurturing of new generations—is indeed profound and meaningful. But here’s where critical thinking becomes essential: just because something is beautiful doesn’t mean it defines a person’s entire value, purpose, or happiness.

This statement, while emotionally appealing, functions more like a distraction than a justification. It doesn’t actually answer the question—“Are women happier as housewives?” Instead, it redirects the conversation toward biology and emotion, using the undeniable beauty of motherhood to support a much more debatable opinion: that a woman should remain in the home and not work.

Then he goes on to say that having children, taking care of them, and doing their best to be a good mother ultimately makes women the happiest people on the planet. On the surface, this might sound positive or even agreeable. And yes, for many women, motherhood can be one of the most meaningful and joyful parts of life.

However, if we don’t pause to critically analyse this statement, it’s easy to nod along and accept it as a universal truth. But let’s dig deeper.

While it is true that some women find deep happiness and fulfillment in being mothers, the statement falsely implies that this is the only or best path to happiness for all women. That’s a sweeping generalisation, and it ignores the reality that every woman is different—emotionally, financially, and personally.

Even more importantly, the statement presents a false choice: that a woman must choose between being a good mother or having a career. This simply isn't true. A woman can absolutely be a loving, present, and responsible mother and have a career. These two things are not mutually exclusive.

In fact, there are countless working mothers who balance both roles with strength, care, and commitment. Many women work not just out of necessity, but because they are passionate about their fields, want to contribute to society, or find personal fulfillment in their work. To suggest that their happiness or their maternal capability is somehow less valid because they are not full-time homemakers is both disrespectful and inaccurate.

In the next part of the video, he reinforces his earlier claim by saying something along the lines of: “I don’t see the point of a woman slaving away for a corporation that doesn’t care about her, rather than slaving away for her flesh and blood.”

Now this statement may sound protective or even empathetic at first glance—it paints corporations as cold and heartless, and children as deserving of love and sacrifice. But once again, if you don’t critically examine the framing, it becomes easy to accept this without questioning the deeper implications.

First, notice the use of emotionally charged language: “slaving away.” It positions women’s labor in both work and home life as suffering. This language removes the possibility of agency and empowerment. Many women choose to work—not out of oppression, but because they value independence, professional achievement, financial security, and personal growth. It’s not “slavery” when you have control and choice.

Second, by contrasting corporate work with caring for “flesh and blood,” the statement attempts to create a moral hierarchy: that working for your children is noble and selfless, but working for a paycheck is cold and meaningless. But this ignores that:

  • A paycheck feeds those children.
  • A working mother may be investing in her child’s future.
  • Having a career might be the only way a woman escapes poverty, domestic dependence, or abuse.

So to say that working for a company is pointless simply because that company doesn’t love you like your children do is emotional manipulation, not logic.

Moreover, this framing subtly guilt-trips working mothers—suggesting that if they really loved their children, they would stay home. But again, this assumes all women can afford to, want to, or should. It also entirely ignores the role of the father or male partner—why is this burden only placed on women?
